## Summary

This experimental study demonstrates **nanoporous single-layer graphene** as a desalination membrane: oxygen-plasma etching tunes nm-scale pores in monolayer graphene, achieving nearly complete salt rejection with high water permeance. Flux under pressure-driven flow reaches up to ~10⁶ g m⁻² s⁻¹ at 40 °C in reported measurements, while osmotic-driving-force experiments yield lower permeance values. The work situates graphene membranes in the context of prior theoretical predictions of sub-nm pore selectivity and emerging experimental graphene/GO separation literature.

## Methods

- **Fabrication:** Defining pores in monolayer graphene via oxygen plasma etching with tunable pore size.
- **Testing:** Desalination performance (salt rejection, water flux) under pressure and osmotic driving forces.

## Findings

- Strong salt rejection with rapid water transport relative to conventional polymer membranes in the reported experiments.
- Highlights plasma-based pore generation as a practical route to controlled nanopores in single-layer graphene.

## Limitations

- Pore size distribution, long-term fouling/scaling, and manufacturing scalability are not solved in a single study; transport models depend on pore chemistry and hydration structure.
